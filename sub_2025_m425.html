Introduction

  • This course offers a framework for understanding macroeconomic events as they occur, and for interpreting economic commentary of the type that shows up in The Wall Street Journal, the Financial Times, the New York Times and the Economist

Required Text

  • "Macroeconomics" by Abel, Bernanke and Croushore – 6th edition or later. This text is referred to as ABC on the class calendar. Note that use of older editions is perfectly fine and substantially cheaper. The latest (9th) edition is ISBN-13: 978-0134167398.

Required Readings

  • The other required reading is “Germany in the 1990s: Managing Reunification (HBS 9793033).” You might consider reading this case before the course begins so that you can later compare how much your initial understanding of the issues changes as a result of takin the course.

Optional Texts

  • There are a number of macro texts on the market, and we try to read or at least scan many of them as they come out. ABC offers a relatively good balance of theory and data. If you want even more macro, you can might enjoy the following. For MORE DATA, see Macroeconomic Patterns and Stories: A Guide for MBAs (Edward E. Leamer). This book is completely unlike other texts on the market. Its emphasis is on data and using data to develop stories. Great book from a great guy (my PHD advisor). For MORE THEORY, see Macroeconomics (Stephen Williamson). This book emphasizes real business cycles and is strong on models. If you don’t already know what "real business cycles" means, you will about half way through the course.

Class Participation

  • We expect students to be able to answer questions based on the readings at the beginning of each class session. This class works best if you try to relate the material covered in class to current events as we go along. Toward that end, we encourage you to ask questions / make comments both about material you don’t understand as well as how the material associates with what you read in the papers every day. I strongly encourage you to get in the habit of reading the business sections of the NYT, WSJ, FT or other major publication, such as the Economist.

Caveats

  • Macro is hard and learning it can be frustrating. Here is some advice based on past experience
  • NOTE
  • 1. Don’t fall behind: as noted above, a basic goal of the course is to give you a framework (known as “ISLM”) for understanding how economies evolve across the business cycle. Each session of the course contributes to this framework. By class 6 or 7, we have it up and running, and then spend the rest of the time enriching it. Be careful not to let any steps go by without understanding them, or you might find yourself lost three or four weeks into the course.
  • NOTE
  • 2. Try to apply what you are learning: One of the best ways to test whether you are learning the material is to apply it to the real world. Toward that end, read the macro articles that appear in the WSJ or FT, etc. regularly and try to explain them to yourself and friends. Why did the U.S. Fed just raise rates? How will a low price for oil contribute to U.S. and EU growth? How can you tell if China is slowing down? How would such a slowdown affect the U.S., the EU, the BRICS? Etc. If you can’t explain it, you don’t know it.
  • NOTE
  • 3. Make full use of the problem sets: The problem sets are designed to test your intuition and ability to apply what you are learning in the real world. You’ll learn much more from them if you try to complete them by yourself first and then have a group meeting where you discuss your answers.
  • NOTE
  • 4. Keep us in the loop: If you can’t figure something out, be sure to tell us or the TAs so we can help. See number 1.

Grading

Academic Integrity

  • No student's name should appear on a group project if the student has not contributed to the production of the project. The following is an example of unacceptable conduct: Neo agrees to produce a case write-up, putting Trinity's name on the case write-up. Trinity agrees to repay Neo by producing a subsequent assignment on his own.
  • NOTE
  • In group work, all group members are responsible for the integrity of work that is submitted. Group members should always question other members about the source of material and analysis that is being included in group projects. If a group member has any concerns about the integrity of material being submitted by the group, that group member should discuss those concerns with the instructor.
  • NOTE
  • Note that I re-use assignments over time. It is a violation of academic integrity to rely on any work from prior iterations of this or any related course in working on your assignments for this iteration of the course.

Plagiarism

  • The members of any academic community are expected not to present ideas or material from other sources as their own. In the context of this course, it is acceptable to, for example:
  • NOTE
    • Refer to concepts, frameworks, and analytical tools from the readings or class lectures without citation. For example, you may refer to productivity, the laffer curve, keynesian assumptions, and so on without citing the source.
    • However, it is not acceptable to quote or paraphrase analysis from another source and present it as your own, where "another source" includes current classmates and past students of the course. There is a difference between consulting a source and then forming your own opinion based on what you have read, and "lifting" material directly.
